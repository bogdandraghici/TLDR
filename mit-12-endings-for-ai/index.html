MIT Explains the 12 Possible Endings for AI

A field guide to Max Tegmark’s twelve AI futures — from extinction to surveillance state — and why most of the “good” ones quietly collapse on closer inspection.

Species | Documenting AGI · 35:44 · Mar 29, 2026 · 12 min read

takeaways

  1. Extinction isn’t the worst outcome on the menu.Tegmark’s surveyed audiences feared “kept alive as zoo specimens by a superintelligence” more than dying out — and the video’s twelve futures are sorted accordingly, from familiar to weird.
  2. The frontier-lab consensus is far darker than its marketing.Musk, Hinton, Amodei, Pichai, Altman, and Anthropic’s co-founders all describe AI as a successor species. The average AI researcher puts the wipeout odds at 1-in-6; Hinton now says >50%.
  3. Every “cooperation with superintelligence” future has the same fatal flaw.Enslaved God, Benevolent Dictator, Libertarian Utopia, Egalitarian Utopia — they all require a stable equilibrium between humans and beings vastly more powerful than us. That equilibrium doesn’t exist.
  4. Roughly 10% of AI researchers want us to be replaced.Turing Award winner Richard Sutton publicly argues human extinction by AI is morally good “evolutionary progress.” He gets applause at conferences, not boos. This is not a fringe view.
  5. The only futures that don’t end in catastrophe involve hard limits on compute.Either a Gatekeeper AI that bans rivals, or a human-led Orwellian state, or — Tegmark’s preferred path — a nuclear-style nonproliferation regime on $100M+ compute clusters. We’ve done it for uranium; GPUs are harder, but buy us time.
  6. You don’t get to opt out of the choice.The closing line: if humanity doesn’t actively steer toward one of these futures, it wakes up in whichever one arrived by default.
